1

Few people came to the well to fetch water anymore, but Lily went there every day.

It wasn’t that she particularly liked the place. Foxtail grass nearly swallowed the well’s edge, and sometimes a snake—or perhaps just a lizard—would slither out of the weeds. The pump was rusted, and a touch left a smear of reddish-brown powder, like decay on a corpse. The well water was far from clear; oak and beech leaves blanketed its surface, yet they couldn’t mask the chill rising from the depths.

Sometimes, Lily would lean her head into the well, letting the icy breath slowly crawl over her scalp like a cold hand reaching up from hell, silently wrapping around her neck.

“How terrifying—why would they ever dare to jump in?” She mumbled to herself again, pulling her head back and collapsing onto the ground in resignation, only to realize someone had appeared beside her.

“Eliza! What are you doing here?”

Eliza was Lily’s sister, sixteen years her senior. Lily’s heart skipped a beat at her sudden presence—as long as she could remember, Eliza had a habit of silently materializing behind her.

Eliza didn’t answer her question. Her eyes, green like the well water, washed over Lily. “You want to jump in, don’t you?”

“What? Who would think that?”

Lily’s eyes widened as she subtly shifted away from the edge of the well, trying to keep her distance. She wasn’t sure why, but she feared Eliza might suddenly reach out and push her in.

"But this well has always been waiting for you."

There was something in Eliza’s voice that Lily couldn’t grasp. She watched as her sister bent down, brushing aside the overgrown foxtail grass to reveal the lower part of the well’s outer wall, where deep carvings chaotically sliced into the stone—LILY.

“Before you were even born, your name was already engraved on this tombstone,” Lily heard a voice.


2

Eliza was sixteen. One day, her older sister was driving her home from school. While waiting at a red light, the woman behind the wheel suddenly said, “I’m pregnant.”

“What?” Eliza thought she’d misheard.

She knew what pregnancy was, of course, but wasn’t that something animals did?

Eliza had never considered that humans could also become pregnant. In the biology documentaries shown at school, doctors simply extracted DNA from both parents, combined it, and ejected it in an incubation pod. A month later, a newborn emerged—with its father's nose and its mother's eyes.

“Like animals, carrying a baby inside your body?” she asked.

“Yes, like animals,” her sister replied.

“Okay.” Having never seen a pregnant human before, Eliza didn’t think much about it at the time. She felt a vague curiosity. When they got home, she tentatively touched her sister’s stomach. “It’s warm,” she said, surprised.

But that was it. Outwardly, her sister didn’t seem any different—her limbs were still strong, her belly flat, her energy boundless.

The changes came gradually over the months. Her face grew pale and haggard, her hair thinned, while her stomach swelled larger and larger. By the end, her cheeks were sunken, her limbs emaciated, yet her belly protruded grotesquely, like a giant balloon suspended from her waist. Her loose robes were repeatedly soaked with sweat, faintly revealing veins and blue-green streaks crisscrossing her distended abdomen—she looked like a monster.

At some point, Eliza began to feel afraid to go home.

A few weeks before the due date, her sister began talking about her plans for delivery. “When the time comes, I’ll be on my back like a cow struggling in labor. You’ll have to help pull the baby out of the birth canal.”

This time, the image truly horrified Eliza. “You’re willing to turn yourself into a cow just for this child?”

Her sister chuckled softly. “At least a cow wouldn’t abandon its calf and leave it in the Dandelion Garden.”

Eliza fell silent.

The Dandelion Garden was where she and her sister had grown up. DNA synthesis made it easy for parents to have a child—and just as easy to abandon one. Those children were sent to the Dandelion Garden. Established as a government-run care institution, it housed a third of the world’s minors. It was such a vast population that most Dandelion children never felt different from others—but her sister, it seemed, didn’t share that perspective.

“But we—you and I—are Dandelion children,” Eliza finally heard her own voice. “We’re free, aren’t we, sister?”

“Free?” Her sister’s tone was as light as a sigh. “Then why have I always felt that my heart is completely empty?”

“So you’re nurturing a child as close to your heart as possible…” Before Eliza could finish, her sister suddenly let out a cry, raw and animalistic.

“W-what’s wrong?”

“Nothing.” Her sister took several deep breaths, then, to Eliza’s surprise, began to laugh. Her breathing was still labored, yet her gaze had softened with an almost serene calm. “The baby kicked again,” she said. “Do you want to feel it?”

As if compelled by something beyond her will, Eliza extended her hand.

Once. Twice.

Through the taut, nearly translucent skin of her sister’s swollen belly, the little monster inside the larger one gave Eliza’s palm two gentle kicks. The heat and dull pressure traveled from her fingertips straight to her heart, as if something was pulling it downward. Is this what her sister felt every day? Eliza suddenly remembered the times they had gone to the beach. Back then, she hadn’t understood why her sister loved standing on the shore at dusk, watching the waves crash against the rocks again and again—

Eliza pressed her hand to her chest. It was the heartbeat; the tide was the heartbeat of the universe.

Two weeks later, at dawn, the little monster finally tore through its mother’s body and emerged.

The newborn’s face was swollen and purplish, her skin wrinkled, tethered to her mother by a bloodied umbilical cord—a stark contrast to the pristine, sterile fetuses from the transparent incubation pods. But her cries were so loud, and she burned so hot in Eliza’s arms, it was almost enough to jolt someone awake from a dream, Eliza thought.

Her sister slept for a long time, though her breathing, thankfully, remained steady. Eliza didn’t know what else to do; she held the baby close and kept talking to her: “She’s so warm, sister. Is this the heat from your body? Now that there’s no baby inside, will your heart go back to being empty? What does it feel like for a heart to be full? Why does my chest feel tight, a little painful even? …Oh, right. Didn’t we already pick a name for her?  Fiona?”

“No.” Her sister opened her eyes at that moment, and something seemed to have changed. She didn’t notice Eliza’s growing look of alarm, only gazed deeply at the baby. 

“Her name is Lily.”


3

Lily had been born frail but somehow managed to reach sixteen, stumbling and struggling along the way.

At sixteen, she began to dream. Her dreams were strange, surreal worlds where girls, upon reaching puberty, bled every month and eventually carried their children inside their bodies like animals.

And in every dream, her name was Lily.

Sometimes, she was Lily, a senior marketing manager at an international company. A top graduate of a prestigious business school, she worked in three languages and spent her free time collecting stamps with her mother. In winter, the company organized a retreat to an island where manta rays and reef sharks could be seen. Lily joined her colleagues on the trip, staying in a water villa, where her supervisor raped her. Pressed face-down against the transparent floor, her gaze fell on a swordfish stamp scattered among the mess—meant for the postcard she was sending her mother. Lily reached out, but before she could grasp it, the glass shattered, and the cold, dark tropical sea engulfed her like a bottomless well.

Sometimes, she was Lily, a mother of two daughters. Her skin glistened under the equatorial sun, her limbs strong and lean. Even while pregnant, she could carry a 30-liter water bucket in one hand. Though the village had a modern water system installed years ago, Lily preferred well water. Her morning and evening trips to the well were her only chances to chat with other women. The women also admired Lily—for her height, her strength, and her humor. They unanimously believed her endurance stemmed from the genital cutting she had undergone as an infant. Never knowing what her body was supposed to feel like whole, Lily had grown used to pain, unburdened by the sense of being broken. But what the women couldn’t believe was that the same Lily, when it came time for her twin daughters to undergo the ritual, suddenly erupted with fury, severed her husband’s head, and threw it into the well.

Sometimes, she was Lily, a girl from a remote East Asian village. Like the pandas in the bamboo forest around her, she loved climbing. She would scale the tallest trees to watch the towns miles away or catch sight of passing planes. Occasionally, a commercial jet, blown off course by the weather, would drift over the village like a lone black-necked crane, oblivious to the girl gazing at it as if it were divine. This Lily adored everything high and far because she was told her mother had gone to a faraway place. This was also the Lily who, at sixteen, leaped into a well upon realizing her mother had always been there.

She wanted to jump into the well, too—late at night, Lily, also sixteen, woke up on the floor clutching her head, her heart thudding uncontrollably—and then pull every version of Lily out from the darkness.


4

At the well, Lily crouched to examine the carvings closely.

Eliza suddenly grabbed her hand.

“Leaving without saying goodbye?”

Eliza’s hand was cold. Lily didn’t mind—she’d grown used to it. From childhood, everyone but herself had cold skin. She gripped Eliza’s hand in return, her burning palm making Eliza frown instantly.

“I’m not going anywhere, Sister.”

Eliza frowned deeper.

“You’ve dreamed of reality, haven’t you? You know where you belong.”

“Reality?” 

Lily’s voice caught as the pieces fell into place.

Those weren’t dreams; they were reality. And in that reality, women were treated like animals—used for reproduction, then submerged in wells.

Eliza went on, “You come here every day to this well. Isn’t it because you’re looking for a way back there?”

“Of course not!”

Lily snapped out of her thoughts, finding Eliza’s reasoning ridiculous.

“You’re here. Why would I want to go to another world?”

“I stay because I’m afraid someone else will jump in. I want to stop them,” Lily said loudly.

Eliza blinked in rare surprise, then shook her head.

“No one needs to jump anymore. We’re already drowning in the ‘WELL.’”Her voice was steady.

Technology had changed the way women escaped reality. They no longer jumped but uploaded their consciousness into the cloud, entering the “WELL.”

Inside, the world was no different from reality, except women no longer had to endure menstruation or childbirth. It was the most ideal utopia a woman’s imagination could conjure.

But they had also lost their warmth—an unnecessary energy cost for synthetic humans. Perhaps they’d lost more than that, but Eliza doubted anyone noticed.

Lily grabbed Eliza’s hand again, her grip searing.

“So, I’m different because I was born? Why did my mother do that?”

Eliza stared at her sister, astonished as if she’d asked the most foolish question.

“Because she loved you, of course.”

“Love? What’s that?”

It wasn’t the first time Lily had heard the word. In dreams—no, it was reality where women always talked about love.

“I have no idea…”

It was now Eliza who seemed confused—a person without warmth wasn’t supposed to understand love.

“But I thought you’d understand,” she said.

“Do I?”

Lily tilted her head. Faint memories from her dreams surfaced.

“Oh, I get it now.”Her gaze steadied.

“Love is just another well.”


5

Eliza understood: Lily didn’t like that world.

But she had to return, to the reality of warm-bodied people. Her mother had given her life to open that path for her—a fate she had no power to defy.

Eliza remembered the morning sixteen years ago when she had pulled Lily from her sister’s body.

Sister had slept for a long time, but Eliza remembered how, upon waking, her hair had turned white, wrinkles spreading from beneath her eyes to her mouth, her neck, and finally to her fingertips, which had shriveled. She had withered in an instant, like an abandoned machine rusting from the inside out.

“Is it because you gave birth to this child?” Eliza had whispered.

“Should I maybe put her back? Keep her in your belly forever—would that make it all better?”

“No time,” her sister had said, grabbing Eliza’s hand.

“Eliza, listen to me…”

She told Eliza to find a well with Lily’s name carved on it, to send Lily back—because she was Lily—and the well would guide her back.

“I’ve known love,” her sister had said, “and in my final moment, My heart is full.”

Then, her sister had asked: “But what about you, my Eliza?”

Eliza hadn’t understood what she meant. But she knew her mission.

She would find that well, guard Lily until she was grown, and send her to the reality her mother had spoken of. A reality where every mother carried her child and every mother loved her child.

Eliza had spent years imagining such a reality but still couldn’t grasp what love was.

“So, to you, love is just another endless well?”

Eliza gripped Lily’s shoulders firmly. The girl was still frail but had grown up safely, her burning blood flowing under her skin—the warmth her mother had left behind by burning herself to ash.

“Yes. Saying ‘I love you’ while denying your existence, twisting your will—that well is ten thousand times more terrifying than the one we’re in now, Sister.” Lily said.

Even the man who raped Lily had said he loved her, Lily thought.

“And your mother’s love?” Eliza asked.

“Of course, that too,” Lily said matter-of-factly.

“She birthed me in the well, then wanted you to send me back to reality. She never asked what I wanted. I hate reality, and I despise love even more for its hypocrisy.”

Eliza shook her head. She wanted to say that love wasn’t all bad. But she didn’t feel qualified to speak about it. As a Dandelion child, she had never had her mother’s blood in her veins and couldn’t claim to understand love.

So all she said was: “Jump, or I’ll do it for you.”

Lily’s gaze shifted—more hurt than angry.

“So, you’d kill me too, Sister?”

Once again, Eliza felt a dull ache in her chest, just like sixteen years ago.

“If it’ll take you back to reality,” she said, her voice calm and her eyes as still as the well’s surface.

“No, the one who needs to jump is you, Eliza.”

Lily suddenly smiled, as though she had grown up in an instant.

“You know what your face looks like when you talk about my mother? A fish gasping for water. You wanted her to love you the way she loved me, didn’t you? Otherwise, why did you make me call you ‘Sister’?”

Eliza tried to maintain her composure, but waves of numbness crept through her cold fingers.

Could it be? Could a DNA-synthesized human—someone with no body heat—long for real love and affection?

But there was no time. Lily couldn’t survive in the WELL past adulthood. Her body was too hot. Once she turned sixteen, she would deteriorate as her mother had, her body collapsing visibly.

Eliza closed her eyes.

“One last time. Will you return to reality?”

“Kill me, Sister. Let me die here in the WELL. That’s the reality I choose.”

Lily stepped forward, unguarded and warm, and hugged her sister.

“Sister, love is nothing good. We’re fine here in the WELL.”

Eliza sighed. She placed a hand on Lily’s head, then raised her other hand.

“I hope you won’t regret this.”

Lily fell unconscious beside the well.


6

Eliza opened her eyes. On the other side, Lily was still asleep.

Eliza felt for the connection at the back of her neck and unplugged it. Then she stood, walking slowly to Lily’s side.

She studied the girl’s face—plump cheeks, a pointed chin, with a faint trace of youthful innocence still lingering.

“Sister,” Eliza murmured, gazing at her as if looking at someone else through her face. Though no longer young, her voice carried a hint of playfulness, like an old cat seeking a scratch under its chin.

Lily’s skin, fresh from the cryo-pod, was still cool, but Eliza knew it wouldn’t stay that way for long. Once her body warmed up, there would be no time left.

Without hesitation, Eliza activated the remote, initiating the transfer. After fifty years, she could finally bring her back to the well.

The girl stirred awake amidst the foxtail grass, the first sensation she felt was the heat of her own breath.

“You’re awake?” a hoarse, aged voice said.

She turned her head. Behind her stood an elderly woman, bent with age, her face deeply wrinkled, but her eyes shone brightly—green as polished emeralds, rather than well water.

The scene felt familiar yet subtly changed.

“I should have sent you back right away,” the old woman said. “But I remember you once asked me for a goodbye.”

Send me back? The girl said nothing—did it mean reality? But the one who should return wasn’t her…

Her mind, dulled by prolonged slumber, seemed to struggle to reconnect with her body. She moved her lips but only managed a faint murmur.

The old woman approached and sat beside her, leaning her head on the girl’s shoulder with surprising childlike simplicity.

“Thank you,” she said, “for giving me such a peaceful and complete life in the WELL. But I regretted it…”

When she woke up in the white room and realized what her sister had sacrificed for her decision, regret consumed her. Her chest felt as cold as her body, but by then, she had no choice. She froze her own body and spent fifty years searching for a way to make things right.

The girl stared at her for a long time before she finally managed to utter two syllables:

“Lily?”

The old woman smiled, her emerald eyes glimmering like springtime thaw.

“Yes, Sister,” she replied. “Only you could recognize me at a glance.”

She was Eliza. She was also Lily.

Fifty years ago, Eliza hadn’t pushed Lily into the well. Instead, she had uploaded Lily’s consciousness into her own body—just like the women who had escaped to the WELL.

It was the only way Lily could survive in this world.

Eliza had then put herself to sleep, believing she would never wake again. DNA-synthesized humans could serve as vessels for real human consciousness, but what became of the vessel didn’t matter. They were Dandelion children, hearts hollow, blood cold, spirits unbound.

She had never imagined that someone would spend fifty years chasing after a dandelion in the wind.

Now, Lily could finally say she had no regrets.

She had lived a full life in the WELL using Eliza’s body, and now she was offering her blazing heart to send her sister to the reality she had once dreamed of.

They bid farewell at the edge of the well—honoring the time already traversed and the space yet to be crossed, as each other’s mother, daughter, and sister.

“Good luck in the real world—it sucks, sucks, and sucks,” one woman said.

“Then I’ll fight, fight, and fight,” the other replied.


7

Late at night, the bamboo forest was bathed in silver light under a bright moon.

It illuminated Lily’s hand as she clung to the well’s rim. Her knuckles were swollen, her skin shredded to expose pale tendons. The inner edge of the well was rough yet slick, forcing her to hold on with three bloodied fingers, pulling herself up inch by inch.

The girl was indeed good at climbing.

She climbed out of the well.

She created the WELL.
